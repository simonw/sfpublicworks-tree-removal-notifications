  • District 5
    390 Page St.
    Site Number : 2
    Reason(s) for Removal : Permit no. 788915. As part of the Page Street Neighborway Project, the entire intersection will be raised and sidewalk bulbs constructed at all returns of the Page Street and Buchanan Street intersection. These sidewalk extensions will reduce crossing distances for pedestrians and intend to make pedestrians more visible to approaching vehicles. The main goal of this project is to improve pedestrian safety in a corridor with high pedestrian volumes, in line with the City’s Vision Zero policy to reduce pedestrian fatalities.
    https://www.visionzerosf.org/about/what-is-vision-zero/
    There is an existing tree at the Northeast return that is located in the middle of the future widened sidewalk. Below are pedestrian safety concerns presented by the tree’s location.
    • The tree is in the view cone of the approaching driver. As a result, the tree obstructs visibility of pedestrians that are waiting at the top of the curb ramp from vehicles traveling westbound on Page Street. This greatly reduces the reaction time between pedestrians and drivers. This the main safety issue the improvements at this intersection intend to address.
    • There is an existing wooden light pole that further obstructs visibility for pedestrians. This creates a “telephone pole effect” where the two objects merge from the vantage point of a driver and effectively form one large obstruction jointly. This makes it even more important to keep this sidewalk clear of obstructions to ensure pedestrians are visible to vehicles.
    • Pedestrian visibility is especially important since high volumes of children are anticipated in this area due to the project’s proximity to a Koshlan Community Park and Learning Garden, which is a nearby school for at-risk youth.
    • The existing tree obstructs the illumination provided by the street light which further reduces the visibility of pedestrians at night. Statistically most pedestrian collisions occur at night and having less visibility makes it even more difficult for vehicles to see people waiting to cross. Additionally, people with low vision need illumination to navigate the sidewalk and the tree’s presence makes it more difficult for individuals with vision impairments to find their way.
    • The tree is in conflict with the proposed curb ramp geometry. This curb ramp is needed to allow individuals with disabilities, including wheelchair users, to access the sidewalk and crosswalks.
    • Due to the small size of the tree well, if this tree were to remain, it would likely heave the sidewalk as the tree roots grow. This damage to the sidewalk may create trip hazards for pedestrians and other non-accessible conditions.
    The City makes every effort to preserve existing trees. However, the health safety and welfare of the public is paramount and is the City’s highest obligation. After thorough evaluation of the pedestrian safety considerations at this intersection, and review by Bureau of Urban Forestry staff, the City has determined that this tree should be removed. Appraised value of tree will be assessed; no room for replacement on site.
    Posting period: 12/6/2021 - 1/5/2022

    The Folsom-Howard Streetscape Project
    Reason(s) for Removal : The Folsom-Howard Streetscape Project will make the South of Market Neighborhood (SoMa) a safer and more inviting place to walk, bike, shop, and take transit. Both Folsom Street and Howard Street are a part of San Francisco's "High-Injury Network" which is the 12 percent of city streets that account for 70 percent of San Francisco's severe and fatal traffic crashes. In 2018, two people were killed on Folsom and Howard. The Folsom-Howard Streetscape Project will improve safety on these corridors and help the City meet its Vision Zero goal of eliminating traffic fatalities. The Folsom-Howard Streetscape Project completed the planning phase and conducted in-depth public outreach; the project was approved by the SFMTA board in Spring 2019. Several near-term safety upgrades were completed on both corridors over the last three years. Some outreach will continue to take place to inform the community of final public realm treatments. Folsom Street is currently in detailed design and is scheduled to start construction in 2022. Howard Street will start detailed design soon after Folsom Street begins construction; though the schedules are staggered to address budgetary needs and construction impacts, we continue to address the two corridors together and will focus on Howard Street after meeting the critical milestones for Folsom Street.

    The SFMTA will schedule this item for a public hearing. Please visit their website for further information at: https://www.sfmta.com/projects/folsom-howard-streetscape-project
